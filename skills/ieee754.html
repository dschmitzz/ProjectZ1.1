IEEE-754 Floating Point

The IEEE-754 standard defines a 32-bit floating point number as 1 sign bit, 8 exponent bits, and a 23-bit mantissa.

The decimal number is equal to (-1)sign×2exponent - 127×1.mantissa

So valid exponents can range from -126 to 127

Converting decimal portion to binary

  • Keep multiplying by 2 until you reach 1 or you use up 23 bits.
  • If the product < 1, append a 0 bit.
  • If the product ≥ 1, append a 1 bit and truncate.
  • (e.g. 23/64 × 2 = 23/32 0
  • 23/32 × 2 = 23/16 1
  • 7/16 × 2 = 7/8 0
  • 7/8 × 2 = 7/4 1
  • 3/4 × 2 = 3/2 1
  • 1/2 × 2 = 1 (terminate) 1
  • ⇒ 23/64 = .0101112)

Converting decimal to IEEE

  1. Convert the truncated decimal to binary (e.g. 3.75 →3=112)
  2. Convert the decimal portion to binary (e.g. .75=.112)
  3. Write the whole binary number in the form of truncated.fraction (e.g. 3.75 = 11.112)
  4. Move the decimal point left until there is exactly one 1 left of the decimal. (e.g. 11.11→1.111, 1 shift)
  5. The original number in scientific notation is the new number * 2#shifts (e.g. 3.7510 = [1.111×21]2)
  6. Choose the sign bit: 0 if nonnegative, 1 if negative (e.g. 1.111×21 = 0 xxxxxxxx xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx)
  7. Get exponent bits and convert to 8-bit binary: exponent = #shifts + 127 (e.g. exponent = 127 + 1 = 128 = 1000 00002)
  8. Get mantissa bits: take the portion after the decimal of scientific notation to 23 bits (e.g. .111→1110000000000000000000)
  9. Combine the sign bit, exponent bits, and mantissa (e.g. 0 10000000 1110000000000000000000)

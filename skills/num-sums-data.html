Numerical Summaries of Data

Sample Mean

Given \(n\) observations in a sample denoted \(x_1, x_2, ..., x_n,\) the sample mean (average) is $$\bar{x} = {\sum_{i=1}^n{x_i} \over n} = {x_1 + x_2 + ... + x_n \over n} $$

Quartiles

A dataset has three quartiles which partition it into four equal segments:

  1. First quartile (lower quartile), \(q_1 = {n+1 \over 4}\), greater than \(25\)% of the data.
  2. Second quartile (median), \(q_2 = {n+1 \over 2}\), greater than \(50\)% of the data.
  3. Third quartile (upper quartile), \(q_3 = {3(n+1) \over 4}\), greater than \(75\)% of the data.

The interquartile range, \(\text{IQR} = q_3 - q_1\), measures variability of the data.

Percentiles

Similar to quartiles, percentiles partition a dataset into \(100\) equal segments.

The \(i\)th percentile of a dataset is greater than \(i\)% of the data, where \(i\) is an integer on the interval \([0, 99]\).

In discrete analysis, there is no \(100\)th percentile because it is impossible for any datum to be greater than itself.

Note that \(q_1\), \(q_2\), and \(q_3\) are respectively the \(25\)th, \(50\)th, and \(75\)th percentiles.

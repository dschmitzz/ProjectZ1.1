Frequency Distributions & Histograms

Frequency Distribution

A frequency distribution divides a dataset into intervals (called class intervals, cells, or bins) and counts the number of data in each interval.

An interval's frequency is the number of data in that interval.

An interval's relative frequency is its frequency divided by the total number of observations.

An interval's cumulative relative frequency is the sum of its relative frequency and all relative frequencies of lower intervals.

Histogram

A histogram is a graph of a frequency distribution. To construct a histogram,

  1. Label the intervals on the horizontal scale.
  2. Label the vertical scale with frequencies or relative frequencies.
  3. Draw a rectangle above each interval as tall as that interval's frequency.

Histograms with Unequal Interval Sizes

In a dataset with tight clusters, it is helpful to have smaller intervals in concentrated regions.

In these histograms, rectangle area, not height, is proportional to interval frequency: $$\text{Rectangle height} = {\text{interval frequency} \over \text{interval size} } $$

Categorical Histograms

There are two types of categorical data:

  • Ordinal categories follow an ordered sequence.
  • Nominal categories have no natural order, they are simply different.

A Pareto chart is a histogram whose categories are listed from highest frequency to lowest frequency. It looks like a downward slope.
